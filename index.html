Beneath the Surface

The Belize Barrier Reef: Your Underwater Playground

No trip to Belize is complete without getting in the water. The Belize Barrier Reef is the second-largest reef system on Earth and a UNESCO World Heritage Site, stretching roughly 190 miles down the coast — and in the north it sits just a few hundred meters offshore, so you're floating over living coral within minutes of leaving the dock.

The signature day trip runs out of San Pedro (Ambergris Caye) or Caye Caulker to Hol Chan Marine Reserve and Shark Ray Alley. Hol Chan — Mayan for "little channel" — funnels marine life through a cut in the reef, so a single stop can serve up green turtles, spotted eagle rays, groupers, moray eels and clouds of tropical fish.

See Nurse Sharks on the Belize Barrier Reef

At Shark Ray Alley you'll slip into shallow, crystal water and snorkel calmly beside harmless nurse sharks and southern stingrays — one of Belize's most jaw-dropping, family-friendly experiences, guided every step of the way.

Beyond the Classics

More Reef, Fewer Crowds

Ready for more than the headline sites? Mexico Rocks and Coral Gardens offer gentler, shallower snorkeling ideal for beginners and kids, while the outer atolls — Turneffe, Glover's and Lighthouse Reef — deliver world-class visibility for those willing to travel farther. Belize is one of only a handful of places on the planet with true atolls, and all three in the Western Hemisphere sit right here.

For the full spot-by-spot breakdown, our sister guide to the best snorkeling in Belize covers each reef, what lives there, and the season-by-season conditions that make or break a trip. Insider tip: in most of Belize you can't just wade in off the beach — the reef is offshore, so a guided boat tour with a strong operator is the way to go.

Inland & Ancient

Jungle Rivers, Sacred Caves & Maya Pyramids

Belize's interior is every bit as thrilling as its coast. Near San Ignacio in the Cayo District, you can spend a morning drifting through cave-tubing caverns on the Caves Branch River and an afternoon zip-lining 80 feet over the forest floor — the classic "go high and low" combo that guides love to run together.

For the serious adventurer, the Actun Tunichil Muknal (ATM) Cave is unforgettable: you swim and wade through an underground river to reach chambers the ancient Maya used for ceremonies, where pottery and crystallized skeletal remains still rest where they were left more than a thousand years ago. Access is limited and guide-only, so book early.

Climb into Maya history

Above ground, Xunantunich rewards the climb up El Castillo with sweeping views into Guatemala, while Caracol — once home to over 100,000 people — hides the Sky Palace (Caana), still the tallest structure in Belize at around 43 meters. Pair a ruins visit with cave tubing for a single, jam-packed day inland.

Good to Know

Belize Adventures: FAQ

What is the #1 adventure to do in Belize?
Snorkeling the Belize Barrier Reef — the second-largest reef on Earth — is the signature Belize adventure. Hol Chan Marine Reserve and Shark Ray Alley let you swim over living coral alongside sea turtles, eagle rays and gentle nurse sharks, usually a short boat ride from San Pedro or Caye Caulker.
Which tour company do you recommend most?
Inland & Sea Adventures in San Pedro is our #1 pick. They took our own team out on the Belize Barrier Reef for a Hol Chan and Shark Ray Alley snorkel tour and it was an unforgettable, expertly guided day on the water.
Can you really swim with sharks in Belize?
Yes. Shark Ray Alley, part of Hol Chan Marine Reserve, is famous for snorkeling with harmless nurse sharks and southern stingrays in shallow, clear water. It's safe, guided and family-friendly.
Is cave tubing worth it?
Absolutely. You float on an inner tube down the Caves Branch River through cathedral-like caverns once used by the ancient Maya, often paired with zip-lining. It's a top land adventure and great for families.
What is the ATM Cave?
Actun Tunichil Muknal is a sacred cave where you wade through an underground river to reach Maya ceremonial chambers with pottery and crystallized remains. Access is guide-only and capped daily, so book early.
Which Maya ruins should I climb?
Xunantunich (reached by a hand-cranked ferry, with views into Guatemala) and Caracol (home to Caana, the tallest structure in Belize) are the standouts. Both pair well with a cave or jungle day.
When is the best time to visit Belize?
The dry season from late November to April brings the calmest seas and clearest reef visibility. May to June is quieter and warmer with better deals — just watch for occasional sargassum on some beaches.
Do I need to know how to swim to snorkel?
Basic comfort in water helps, but life jackets and flotation are provided, and good guides stay close. Shallow sites like Coral Gardens and Mexico Rocks are ideal for beginners and kids.
